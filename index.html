Part 1 · System Architecture

02Firmware architecture

RTOS: Zephyr via the nRF Connect SDK. Maintained BLE controller and nrfx drivers; native_sim runs the app on the host for CI; MCUboot + SMP/mcumgr gives signed DFU out of the box. Six sensor streams + BLE + offline logging + DFU is an RTOS problem — bare-metal wins on RAM but loses at this complexity.

Portable C11 core

No Zephyr headers, no malloc. One overwrite-oldest ring per channel, a monotonic 32-bit sequence number per record, a per-channel drop counter.

~40-line port layer

The entire RTOS dependency: opaque sm_lock_t + sm_time_now() — k_spinlock/uptime on Zephyr, mutex/CLOCK_MONOTONIC on host, making concurrent-drain CI-testable.

BLE is a consumer

Drivers never call BLE code; the BLE thread bulk-copies N records on a connection event — the radio decides when the radio runs. Kills priority-inversion bugs.

PPR split (headline)

Run the always-on acquisition loop on the RISC-V PPR while the M33 sleeps in System OFF. Cost: cross-core IPC + shared-SRAM coherency. Open question: does PPR beat PPI/DMA EasyDMA? A first EVK measurement.

Ring buffers are the only shared state; critical sections are index arithmetic + one memcpy, target < 20 µs hold under the BLE guard time.

03Hardware architecture

Rigid-flex PCB: rigid islands (MCU+radio, PPG+optics, battery/charge) wrapped into the band. Every slow sensor hangs off one shared I²C bus (TWIM21); a burst-FIFO sensor moves to SPI. Board area and component height constrain everything.

The single shared bus is the fragile point — one wedged device (brownout, ESD, a clock-stretch hang) takes down all sensing, and it's the same root as Issue A. Rev 2 moves the PPG to its own SPI bus (isolation + throughput), and firmware adds I²C bus recovery (clock-pulse unstick → TWIM reset → per-transaction timeout → re-init) plus per-sensor power gating, so a hung device self-heals and is flagged instead of bricking the whole ring.

Antenna — the dominant RF problem

A finger millimetres away detunes the antenna and eats several dB. Hard keep-out; tune with a finger phantom; ceramic chip antenna on curved flex.

PPG on its own filtered rail

BLE PA transients coupled into a µV photodiode mimic a physiological signal. Separate LDO, star ground; schedule PPG around radio events.

Power tree + rail gating

nRF internal DC/DC; load switches gate VDD_SENSOR_DIG / VDD_ANALOG / VDD_MOTOR. Three sensors leaking 3 µA in "standby" is 7.5% of the week budget.

Thermal — the temp sensor lies

It reads a mix of skin, MCU self-heating, PPG LEDs and motor. Isolate it (flex neck, slotted copper); blackout sampling around PPG bursts and haptics. Trustworthy for trends and deltas only.

Also specified: contact-pad charging (TVS + wet-detect), a fuel-gauge IC (~2 µA), every sensor INT on a wake-capable GPIO.

04BLE interface design

Standard SIG services plus a custom Ring Data Service: a build-time-generated capability descriptor, stream control, notify-based data, config, a control point, log/backfill.

Batching is the whole design. Bandwidth is a non-issue (600 B/s); wakeups are what the battery pays for. With DLE and a 247-byte MTU (244 B payload), the encoder packs a 16-byte header + 14-byte IMU records = 16 per notification — one notify every 320 ms, not 50/s. Batch size derives from the negotiated MTU, asserted in payload_budget_yields_sixteen_imu_samples.

Security: LESC + bonding, reject legacy pairing, resolvable private addresses with rotation (a static MAC is a tracking beacon). Offline logging with backfill-on-reconnect; DFU is MCUboot dual-slot, signed but unlocked with a replaceable key.

05Power management strategy

Two budgets against a 20 mAh usable cell: ~119 µA for 1 week (168 h) and ~833 µA for 24 h.

The assumption

20 mAh is optimistic. A public Oura Ring 5 teardown puts its cell at 10.2 mAh; there the budgets become ~61 µA and ~425 µA, and the week-mode design would deliver under 4 days, not 7.7. 

PPG dominates. PPG energy ≈ LED current × pulse width × sample rate, all firmware-controllable across two orders of magnitude. Levers, best first: (1) adaptive sampling gated on the IMU — measure HR only when still; (2) closed-loop LED drive to the minimum current clearing SNR for this finger; (3) duty-cycle the LED. Shaving 5 µA off the CPU is meaningless while PPG spends 400.

Mechanism: Zephyr PM, tickless idle, M33 in System OFF + RAM retention, interrupt-driven wakeups aligned to connection events. Honest result: estimated research mode is ~878 µA → 22.8 h, missing 24 h by ~5% — fixed by trimming PPG duty or gating the gyro, not claimed until measured.

06Future sensor expansion

Sensors register into a static table; the BLE capability characteristic is generated from it at build time. Adding a sensor is a table entry plus a driver — the GATT layout doesn't change, the version doesn't bump, the app needs no coupled release. It reads the descriptor at connect (sensor_id / record_type / record_size) and renders generically or ignores unknown types — an app built before the sensor existed keeps working.

  • Bus allocation — FIFO/burst parts on SPI, ≤ 10 Hz parts on I²C; reserve spare GPIOs and an I²C address block.
  • Devicetree overlays per variant — the table populates from devicetree, so the image self-describes.
  • Forward-compatible TLV framing — type-tagged records make unknown types skippable, not fatal; never reuse a tag.
  • Board area is the real limit — reserve footprints and INT routing now; keep the antenna keep-out sacred.

07Battery-life trade-offs — 24-hour vs 1-week

Not two settings of one product — two products sharing a BOM. The gap is roughly 7× in average current, spent almost entirely on PPG and radio duty cycle: data density traded for time.

Drift-free rate control

next_due advances by exact periods — 50 Hz means 500 samples per 10 s forever, not 498 after an hour; a long stall is clamped (SM_RATE_MAX_CATCHUP) and reported in missed_deadlines.

Overwrite-oldest, never silent

Every record carries a sequence number, every buffer a drop counter, both sent over BLE — the phone quantifies loss, never interpolates.

Interrupt-masking, not a mutex

The producer is an ISR and a mutex deadlocks there — the embedded port masks interrupts (PRIMASK save/restore) so the lock composes in an already-masked region.

Bulk drain, not per-sample

The BLE layer takes the lock once, copies up to N records in one or two memcpy spans, releases — worst-case hold bounded by bytes moved, sized under the guard time.

Validated on a digital twin

The core sources (sensor_manager.c, sm_ringbuf.c, ble_batch.c) compile byte-for-byte unmodified — the same files the host tests build — and run on a cycle-accurate nRF54L15 simulation through a real sm_port. The chip model boots unmodified upstream Zephyr v4.4, and every sensor (BMI270, MAX30102, TMP117, DRV2605) enumerates by reading its real ID register over I²C. Beyond a clean boot it shows graceful failure: phone out of range, the BLE consumer stalls, the ring overflows, the newest 100 are kept, the exact loss reported.

Part 3 · Debugging & Investigation

Method for all three: cheapest-and-most-discriminating experiment first; reproduce deterministically before changing anything; bisect (electrical vs firmware vs algorithmic), one variable at a time. Instrument first, hypothesize second, fix third.

09Issue A — PPG unreliable as BLE throughput rises

Something scaling with radio activity is corrupting an analog chain. Three separable classes: conducted (power-rail coupling), radiated (2.4 GHz rectified in the AFE), temporal (CPU/IRQ starvation → FIFO overflow). One test partitions them: replace the radio with an equal CPU busy-load, radio off — persists = temporal, vanishes = electrical.

Reproduced on the digital twin

The twin reproduces the digital/timing cause. A BLE connection event is modelled as a periodic SysTick that monopolizes the CPU; the busy CPU can't drain the PPG FIFO, which fills on its own clock and overflows. One flag flips PPG FIFO overflow from 0 → 31 (saturated).

Honest boundary: the twin models the digital cause (CPU starvation → FIFO overflow), not the analog one — radio-TX spikes into the PPG front end, which a digital simulator can't represent.

Tools

Scope AC-coupled on the analog rail; H-field probe for RF; PPK II for burst current; Saleae + GPIOs in the PPG ISR and at radio-active; SystemView for IRQ timing; RTT logging, never UART (UART perturbs the timing under test); a BLE sniffer to phase-lock corruption to on-air events.

Design changes

  • Dedicated filtered analog rail + LDO for the AFE — PSRR checked at ~kHz, not the datasheet's 1 kHz headline.
  • PPG on dedicated SPI with DMA + FIFO-watermark burst reads, so acquisition stops depending on the CPU.
  • Run acquisition on the RISC-V PPR with hardware timestamps (or timer-capture on DRDY); reject high-jitter samples over a confidently wrong HR.

10Issue B — battery life ~40% below expectation

"40% low" means nothing unless "expected" was measured. The expectation is a ~119 µA spreadsheet of guessed duty cycles and datasheet typicals. First suspect: the model — an afternoon to audit, often the actual bug. 40% is also suspiciously close to "one subsystem you forgot."

Likely root causes

  • B1 MCU never reaches budgeted deep-sleep — peripheral still clocked, HFCLK never released, or a debugger left attached (the classic false alarm).
  • B2 Sensor in standby-not-shutdown, a rail never gated, or an ESD-diode back-powering a gated-off rail.
  • B3 Negotiated BLE interval ≠ requested — the phone applies its own policy; excess wakeups / per-sample notifications.
  • B4 The battery — overstated capacity, high ESR, protection-IC quiescent, aging.

Debugging: incremental attribution, not the total. Detach the debugger, measure a bare-board floor, then add one subsystem at a time — clocks, each sensor, advertising, connected, full app — each delta against its model line. Average over a full duty cycle (a once-a-minute 20 mA burst hides in a 100 ms snapshot). Read the negotiated interval on air; validate the cell on a cycler.

Tools

PPK II or Joulescope/SMU for long µA-resolution captures, GPIO markers, Zephyr pm_state residency counters, a BLE sniffer, a battery cycler.

Design changes

  • GPIO state audit + load-switch rail gating as an automated test — every pin in its intended state per mode, bus pins driven low before gating to close the ESD-diode path.
  • Power-state residency counters reported over BLE — turns Issue B from a lab problem into a fleet statistic.
  • A CI power regression test on real hardware with a PPK — the actual fix; fails the build when the sleep floor or cycle-average exceeds a threshold. Add shunts / 0 Ω rail-cut links so any rail is measurable in isolation.

11Issue C — 12% first-pass manufacturing failure

12% is meaningless until decomposed: one mode at 11% is a fixable defect; twelve at 1% each is a design-margin problem. First — Pareto the failures by step, station, shift, panel position, and lot; usually collapses the problem an order of magnitude. Second — the crucial split: bad product or bad test? Retest every failure; if a meaningful fraction passes, suspect the fixture. A Gage R&R quantifies it — a known-good unit failing 5% of the time is most of your 12%.

Likely root causes

  • C1 Fixture/pogo-pin contact, or tester firmware/timing marginality.
  • C2 Unshielded RF test near factory Wi-Fi — the leading test-side suspect for a 2.4 GHz product.
  • C3 Limits set by wish not process spread, Cpk < 1.33.
  • C4 Solder defects (tombstoning, head-in-pillow under the CSP), flex-bonding on a curved substrate, or antenna detuning after final assembly.

Debugging: get parametric data, not pass/fail — the highest-leverage change. A measured value per step per serial catches a distribution drifting toward a limit, separates a margin tail from a defect population, and makes Cpk computable; if the station isn't logging it against serials, fix that first. Then physical FA (X-ray, cross-section, AOI), and check whether failures cluster in time (process shift) or are uniform (design margin).

Tools

X-ray / cross-sectioning, AOI/AXI data, a shielded RF chamber, boundary-scan/SWD structural test, a parametric-histogram-per-step dashboard, DOE once a candidate cause is found.

Design changes

  • DFT/DFM + serial-number traceability — test points and a parametric BIST over SWD, tracked panel → final assembly.
  • Guard-banded limits from measured Cpk, and shielded RF test (non-negotiable at 2.4 GHz in a factory).
  • Staged testing — test the bare board first; a defect reaching final test costs ~10× one caught at board test.

12Assumptions, risks & unknowns

The load-bearing ones:

Assumptions

A1 20 mAh usable cell — likely optimistic (Oura 5 ≈ 10.2 mAh); budgets scale linearly and week mode could fall under 4 days. A2 all currents are estimates — datasheets can move any line ±3×. A3 PPG is the dominant consumer. A4 the app team accepts a capability-descriptor contract. A5 ~1.5 MB RRAM suffices for offline logging. A6 the internal DC/DC needs no external buck.

Top risks

High: antenna detuned by the finger → poor range (finger-phantom tuning from day one); research mode misses 24 h; skin temp corrupted by self-heating; PPG motion artifact during activity. Medium: PPR/M33 shared-memory corruption on version skew (layout-hash gate); motor transients browning out PPG; iOS/Android parameter divergence; charge-pad corrosion/ESD.

Unknowns — and how I'd resolve each

  • Does PPR actually beat PPI + DMA on the M33? Instrument both with a PPK II under identical load; decide on the mAh. Gates the §1.3 split.
  • Real PPG energy per measurement at the SNR needed, across skin tones — decides whether either budget is achievable.
  • Cold-start vs standby crossover per rail-gated sensor; actual regulator/leakage quiescent on assembled hardware (can be 3× a guess).
  • RRAM left after Zephyr + BLE + MCUboot dual-slot, hence whether offline logging needs external flash; lock-hold vs BLE guard time.
